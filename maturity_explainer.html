Foundation & Awareness

1. Clear Business Reason

Is there a clear business reason (i.e., solving a specific problem) driving this Lean Programme?

Define your “True North” — the compelling problem you are solving.

Don’t start until you can state the business problem.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

Before starting any journey, you need a destination. A Lean journey is no different. Starting Lean without a clear business reason is like setting sail without a map—you’ll be busy, but you won’t get anywhere meaningful. The business reason is your “True North.” It answers the critical question: “Why are we doing this?”

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This is the very first and most critical step in the Strategic Alignment phase of the roadmap. The “Business Challenge” is not just a box to tick; it is the foundation upon which the entire transformation is built.

2. Leadership Commitment

Is leadership committed & invested in being involved?

The journey requires active, visible sponsorship from the very top.

It starts with a genuine commitment from leadership.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

A Lean transformation is not a software upgrade or a process change you can delegate to a project team. It is a fundamental shift in the culture and operating philosophy of the entire business. Such a profound change is impossible without the active, visible and unwavering commitment of the most senior leaders.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This is the designated starting point of the entire roadmap. Leadership Commitment is shown as the first foundational element because, without it, none of the subsequent phases are possible. The statement “Leadership is committed to becoming a Lean Company” is the entry ticket for the journey.

3. Foundational Lean Knowledge

Has the team or organisation started acquiring foundational Lean knowledge?

Give everyone a shared language (Value, Waste, Flow).

You can’t eliminate waste until you’ve taught what it is.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

In a Lean transformation, the “tools” are concepts like Value, Waste, Flow, Pull and Continuous Improvement. Foundational knowledge ensures everyone understands these core ideas and can speak the same language. It’s not about making everyone an expert overnight; it’s about giving them the basic literacy to see their own work differently.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

Acquiring this knowledge is the first key activity within the Communication & Training workstream. The initial phase, “Train Steering Committee & Lean Experts,” ensures that the leaders and change agents have this knowledge first so they can effectively lead the transformation.

4. Team Understanding of Goals

How clearly can your team describe what Lean means and how it supports your business goals?

Bridge the gap between “what” Lean is and “why” it matters to the business.

Teach them how the tools help win the game.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

Foundational knowledge teaches a team “what” Lean is. But to truly engage them, they must also understand “why” it matters to them and to the business. This clarity provides context and motivation. It empowers the team to make their own decisions about how to apply Lean principles, because they understand the ultimate goal they are trying to achieve.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This is a crucial outcome of both the Strategic Alignment and Communication & Training phases. When the business challenge is clear and training is effective, teams can directly connect their daily improvement activities to the organization's most important goals.

5. Understanding Lean Concepts

How well does the team understand Lean concepts?

Move from buzzwords to a practical grasp of core principles.

Deep understanding is the key that unlocks real improvement.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

It's one thing to know the names of Lean tools; it's another to deeply understand the principles behind them. This deeper understanding allows teams to go beyond simply "doing" Lean activities and start "thinking" in a Lean way. It enables them to correctly diagnose problems and select the right approach, rather than just applying tools randomly.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This is the primary goal of the "Train Steering Committee & Lean Experts" and subsequent training activities in the Communication & Training phase. The aim is not just awareness, but a robust understanding that allows for effective leadership and problem-solving.

6. Consistent & Ongoing Training

How consistently do team members receive Lean training, and is there a plan for ongoing learning?

Treat learning as a continuous process, not a one-time event.

Build capability through consistent practice and learning.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

A single training session can create initial excitement, but skills and knowledge fade without reinforcement. A plan for ongoing learning ensures that Lean concepts stay fresh, new skills are developed, and the organization's overall capability grows over time. It signals that Lean is not just a temporary project, but a permanent new way of working.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This is a core principle of the entire Communication & Training workstream. It's not just about the initial training, but about establishing a long-term plan for development that supports the Continuous Improvement Cycle.

28. Sharing Lean Success Stories

How consistently are Lean success stories shared to inspire and align the wider business?

Use stories as "lighthouses" to inspire, teach, and build momentum.

Data convinces the mind, but a good story wins the heart.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

A Lean transformation can be a long and challenging journey. Sharing success stories is crucial for maintaining energy and momentum. These stories—of a team that dramatically reduced lead time, or an individual who eliminated a frustrating source of waste—serve multiple purposes. They provide tangible proof that the methods work, they teach others what is possible, and they celebrate the people involved, reinforcing the desired behaviours and inspiring others to join the effort.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

Sharing success stories is a key part of the Communication & Training workstream. It is also an essential leadership responsibility that supports the overall Leadership Commitment, as leaders can use these stories to reinforce the "why" behind the transformation and celebrate progress towards the vision.

Strategy & Structure

7. Defined Lean Structure & Roles

Is there a defined Lean structure or role ownership?

Create clear ownership and a support system for the transformation.

Good intentions need a structure to become reality.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

A Lean transformation cannot be a side job for everyone. To ensure focus and accountability, you need a defined structure. This usually involves establishing a steering committee for high-level guidance, appointing value stream owners who are responsible for end-to-end processes, and identifying Lean experts who can coach and support the teams. This structure provides the engine to drive the transformation forward.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This is the central focus of the Organizational Structure phase of the roadmap. The explicit goal is to "Establish Steering Committee, Value Stream Owners & Lean Experts" to provide the necessary leadership and support system for the entire journey.

8. Strategy Management System

Is a strategy management system in place and regularly reviewed?

Systematically deploy, track, and adapt your strategic goals.

A good strategy needs a system to bring it to life.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

Having a strategy is not enough; you need a system to manage it. A strategy management system (like Hoshin Kanri) is a structured process for ensuring that the high-level strategic goals of the organization are translated into concrete actions and metrics at every level. It involves a regular cycle of planning, deploying, reviewing, and adapting the strategy, ensuring that the entire organization remains aligned and focused on the most important goals.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This is a key component of the Governance & Review Cadence. The system of "Monthly Steering Meetings" and "Quarterly Strategy Reviews" is the practical application of a strategy management system, ensuring that the Lean transformation stays on track and aligned with the business's True North.

9. Cascading Strategic Objectives

How well are strategic objectives cascaded to teams?

Ensure every team knows how their work contributes to the bigger picture.

Alignment happens when strategy is translated, not just broadcast.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

Cascading objectives is the process of breaking down high-level strategic goals into meaningful, actionable objectives for each team and individual. It's not just about telling people what the company goals are; it's about helping them see the direct line of sight between their daily work and the achievement of those goals. This creates powerful alignment and empowers teams to make decisions that support the overall strategy.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This is a direct outcome of a well-run Governance & Review Cadence and an effective Value Stream Workshop. The workshop helps define team-level objectives, and the review cadence ensures they stay aligned with the high-level "Challenge KPI" over time.

Tools & Daily Management

10. Experimenting with Lean Tools

Has the team experimented with any Lean tools on a small scale or in a pilot project?

Start applying the theory in a small, controlled way.

Learning starts when the tools leave the classroom.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

Lean knowledge is best gained through application. Experimenting with tools on a small scale (e.g., trying a 5S exercise in one work cell, or using a Kanban board for a single process) is a low-risk way to build practical skills and confidence. These initial trials provide immediate feedback and help the team learn how the tools work in their specific environment, turning abstract concepts into tangible experience.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This is the very essence of the Pilot Area Transformation. The pilot is designed as a safe space to experiment with tools like "Daily Huddles" and "Problem Solving" for the first time before attempting a wider rollout.

11. Embedding Tools in Daily Work

How embedded and routinely applied are Lean tools in your team's daily work?

Move from occasional use to making Lean tools "the way we work."

Tools become powerful when they become habits.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

The goal is to move beyond using Lean tools as a special "event" and integrate them into the team's daily routine. For example, a daily huddle isn't a special meeting; it's the standard way the team starts its day. A Kanban board isn't a project tool; it's the standard way the team manages its workflow. When tools become embedded habits, continuous improvement becomes a natural, automatic part of the daily work.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This is a key outcome of a successful Pilot Area Transformation. The aim is to make practices like "Daily Huddles" and "Problem Solving" routine within that area, creating a model of embedded Lean that can then be replicated during the Expansion & Integration phase.

12. Tracking & Discussing KPIs

Are Lean-related KPIs tracked and discussed in teams?

Use data to make problems visible and drive conversation.

What gets measured and discussed, gets improved.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) are the vital signs of a process. Tracking them regularly is essential, but the real value comes from discussing them as a team. These discussions, often held at a visual management board, allow the team to see trends, identify when the process is off-track, and trigger a structured problem-solving approach. It shifts the conversation from being based on opinions to being based on facts.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This is a core activity in both the Pilot Area Transformation (through "KPI, Problem Solving") and the overarching Governance & Review Cadence (through "Track Challenge KPI"). It provides the data-driven feedback loop that is essential for continuous improvement.

13. Aligning KPIs to Lean Goals

Are KPIs clearly aligned to Lean goals (flow, quality, waste)?

Measure what matters: the flow of value, not just functional silos.

The wrong KPIs will drive the wrong behaviours, no matter how good your training is.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

Many traditional KPIs measure efficiency within a single department (e.g., machine utilization). While these can be useful, they often encourage behaviours that are bad for the overall system (e.g., building inventory just to keep a machine busy). Aligning KPIs to Lean goals means shifting the focus to measuring things that matter to the customer, such as lead time (flow), first-pass yield (quality), and overall waste reduction. This ensures that improvement efforts are focused on optimizing the whole value stream, not just the individual pieces.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This alignment is a key objective of the Value Stream Workshop & Planning phase. During this workshop, teams analyze the current state and define future state goals that are directly tied to improving flow and quality for the customer. These goals then inform the KPIs that are tracked in the pilot and beyond.

14. Using Daily Huddles

Are daily huddles used consistently to raise and resolve issues?

Create a consistent, daily rhythm for communication and problem-solving.

A daily 15-minute huddle saves hours of firefighting later.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

A daily huddle (or stand-up meeting) is a short, structured meeting where the team reviews performance against the plan, identifies any immediate problems or roadblocks, and coordinates their work for the day. It is a powerful mechanism for creating transparency, fostering team communication, and enabling rapid, real-time problem-solving. It prevents small issues from escalating into major crises and establishes a daily cadence of continuous improvement.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

Daily Huddles are explicitly called out as a key practice to be implemented during the Pilot Area Transformation. They are the cornerstone of a Lean daily management system and are essential for driving the "Problem Solving" and "KPI" tracking activities at the team level.

15. Employee-Driven Improvements

How often do employees suggest and act on improvements?

Tap into the expertise of those closest to the work.

The person doing the job knows more about it than anyone else.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

This is the heart of Kaizen (continuous improvement). It's the belief that the people who perform the work are the true experts and are in the best position to identify problems and develop effective solutions. A culture of employee-driven improvement is one where everyone is not only empowered but expected to suggest and implement improvements as part of their daily job. This unleashes a tremendous amount of creative problem-solving potential throughout the organization.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This culture is the goal of the Continuous Improvement Cycle. The cycle of "Identify Next Problems, Implement Solutions, Evolve the system" is not meant to be driven by managers alone, but by the entire workforce. The "Problem Solving" activities in the Pilot Area Transformation are designed to build this capability from the ground up.

Learning & Scaling

16. Reflecting on Improvements

After an improvement is made, does the team reflect on the outcome to refine the process they just changed?

Systematically learn from every change you make.

Activity without reflection is just busy-ness.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

After an improvement is implemented, it's crucial to pause and reflect. This process, often called "Hansei" in Japanese, involves asking questions like: "Did the change have the effect we expected? Why or why not? What did we learn from this process?" This structured reflection turns every improvement action, whether it succeeded or failed, into a valuable learning opportunity. It prevents teams from repeating mistakes and helps them develop a deeper understanding of their processes.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

Reflection is a key part of the "Evolve the system" step in the Continuous Improvement Cycle. It's also an implicit part of the "Problem Solving" process within the Pilot Area Transformation. Effective reflection ensures that the learnings from one improvement cycle are fed into the next.

17. Capturing & Sharing Learnings

How effectively does the organisation capture and share learnings from one team's improvements so that other teams can benefit?

Turn isolated knowledge into collective intelligence.

Solve a problem once, then share the learning to solve it everywhere.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

When one team solves a difficult problem, that new knowledge is a valuable asset. If it stays with that team, its value is limited. Capturing and sharing learnings means having a systematic way to document what was learned from an improvement activity and disseminate it to other teams who could benefit. This prevents teams from "reinventing the wheel" and accelerates the pace of improvement across the entire organization.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This is a critical enabler for the Expansion & Integration phase. The ability to effectively share learnings from the pilot area is what allows the transformation to scale successfully. It is also a key output of the Continuous Improvement Cycle's "Evolve the system" step.

18. Extending Lean to the Value Chain

Does your Lean philosophy extend across your entire value chain?

See partners as extensions of your own factory.

Your value stream doesn't end at your shipping dock.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

Optimizing your own internal processes is only part of the picture. The total time, cost, and quality experienced by the end customer is determined by the entire value chain, including your suppliers and distributors. Extending Lean means collaborating with these external partners to identify and eliminate waste across organizational boundaries, creating a more efficient and responsive end-to-end system.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This represents an advanced stage of maturity in the Expansion & Integration phase. It involves applying the principles of the Value Stream Workshop not just internally, but to the entire flow of value from raw material to the end customer.

19. Integrating Lean Across Departments

To what extent are Lean practices integrated across departments and partners?

Break down silos to improve the horizontal flow of value.

Don't just improve the silos; improve the flow that connects them.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

Often, the biggest sources of waste and delay in an organization are not within the departments, but in the handoffs between them. Integrating Lean across departments means shifting the focus from optimizing individual functions (vertical silos) to optimizing the end‑to‑end flow of value to the customer (horizontal value streams). This requires cross‑functional collaboration and a shared understanding of the overall process.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This is the core purpose of the Expansion & Integration phase. The goal is to move beyond the pilot area and "Gradually expand Lean Practices to interface areas," breaking down the barriers between functions to improve the overall system.

23. Routine of Reflection & Sharing

How ingrained is the routine for your team to both reflect on improvements and actively share the results?

Make reflection and sharing an ingrained, non‑negotiable habit.

Reflection turns experience into wisdom; sharing makes it capability.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

This is the cultural maturity that combines two previous concepts (Reflection and Sharing) into a single, ingrained routine. It's the point where teams don't need to be reminded to reflect on their work and share what they've learned; it's simply part of their standard work. This routine ensures that learning is continuous and that the organization's collective knowledge is always growing.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This habit is the engine that drives the Continuous Improvement Cycle. A strong routine of reflection and sharing ensures that the cycle is not just a theoretical loop on a diagram, but a living, breathing process that continuously improves and evolves the system.

24. Using Pilot Projects

How often are pilot projects used to test, learn, and adapt Lean improvements?

Use a pilot as a "test track" to learn, adapt, and reduce risk.

Pilots are for learning, not just for implementing.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

A pilot project is a controlled experiment. Its primary purpose is not just to "install" a new process, but to learn. It provides a safe environment to test a new concept, understand its real‑world challenges, adapt the approach based on feedback, and build the capability of the team before attempting a broader, more expensive rollout. Approaching a pilot with a learning mindset is crucial for success.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

The Pilot Area Transformation is the formal application of this concept. It is designed as the organization's first major "test track" for applying Lean principles and tools in a holistic way, with the explicit goal of generating learnings to inform the later Expansion & Integration phase.

25. Spreading Successful Practices

How effectively are successful Lean practices spread across teams or departments?

Take a local success and make it a new company standard.

A great idea, standardized and spread, transforms an organization.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

When a pilot project proves a new practice is effective, the next step is to spread that success. This requires a deliberate process. It involves standardizing the successful practice to make it repeatable, and then actively teaching, coaching, and supporting other teams as they adopt it. This is how an organization takes a single "point of light" from a successful pilot and uses it to illuminate the entire company.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This is the definition of the Expansion & Integration phase. The entire purpose of this phase is to take the successful practices developed in the pilot and "Gradually expand" them to other areas, turning a local improvement into a new organizational capability.

Leadership & Collaboration

20. Leader Presence & Engagement

How present and engaged are leaders in supporting Lean efforts at the workplace?

Go to the "Gemba" (the actual place) to see, learn, and support.

Leaders who lead from the front show that improvement is a priority.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

In Lean, leadership is not about managing from behind a desk or through spreadsheets. It's about being physically present where the work happens (the "Gemba"). This practice allows leaders to observe the real process, understand the real problems, ask insightful questions, and show respect and support for the people doing the work. Visible engagement from leaders is one of the most powerful ways to reinforce that continuous improvement is a core value of the organization.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This directly supports the foundational principle of Leadership Commitment. It is the practical, daily behaviour that brings that commitment to life. It is also essential for effective Governance & Review, as it provides leaders with first‑hand knowledge to guide their strategic decisions.

21. Coaching with Lean Questions

How often do leaders coach and guide teams by asking Lean-focused questions?

Shift from giving answers to building problem‑solvers by asking questions.

A good leader provides answers; a great Lean leader asks questions.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

A traditional manager often acts as the primary problem‑solver, telling their team what to do. A Lean leader acts as a coach, developing the problem‑solving capabilities of their team. They do this by asking guiding questions ("What problem are we trying to solve? What have we tried so far? What did we learn?") rather than providing immediate answers. This Socratic approach builds critical thinking skills and ownership at all levels of the organization.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This is the key leadership skill required to make the Continuous Improvement Cycle and the "Problem Solving" in the Pilot Area Transformation successful. It is how leaders actively participate in and enable the improvement process without taking it over.

22. Culture of Safe Experimentation

To what extent does our organization combine a psychologically safe culture with a structured process to drive effective experimentation?

Combine psychological safety with a structured process (PDCA).

Innovation happens with a safe space to fail and a process to learn.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

Continuous improvement is, by its nature, a process of experimentation. Not every experiment will succeed. A culture of safe experimentation requires two things: 1) Psychological safety, where people feel secure enough to propose new ideas and try things without fear of blame if an experiment "fails." 2) A structured process (like PDCA) that ensures every experiment, successful or not, generates valuable learning. These two elements create a powerful engine for innovation and resilience.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This culture is the soil in which the Continuous Improvement Cycle grows. It is also fundamental to the Pilot Area Transformation, which is itself a large‑scale, safe experiment. It requires both Leadership Commitment to foster safety and Communication & Training to teach the structured process.

26. Cross‑Functional Collaboration

How well do teams from different functions collaborate to improve shared processes?

The race is won or lost in the quality of the handoffs between teams.

Don't coach the runners; coach the handoffs.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

Value is created horizontally across an organization, flowing through multiple teams and departments on its way to the customer. Cross‑functional collaboration is the practice of bringing people from these different departments together to work on improving that entire flow. It requires breaking down departmental silos and fostering a shared sense of ownership for the end‑to‑end process.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This is a key enabler for the Value Stream Workshop, which by nature is a cross‑functional event. It is also the primary behaviour that needs to be fostered during the Expansion & Integration phase to ensure that improvements are made to the whole system, not just within isolated areas.

27. Joint Improvement with Partners

How actively do you execute joint improvement projects with external partners?

Move from negotiation to active, collaborative problem‑solving.

Improve together to win together.

The Big Picture: Teaching the Concept

This is the next level of maturity beyond basic collaboration. It means moving from a traditional, often adversarial, customer‑supplier relationship to a true partnership focused on mutual benefit. It involves actively working together on joint improvement projects, sharing information transparently, and co‑solving problems that exist in the interfaces between the organizations. The goal is to optimize the entire value chain for the benefit of all parties.

Connecting to the Lean Roadmap

This represents the most advanced form of Expansion & Integration, where the scope of improvement extends beyond the company's own walls. It requires a very mature Continuous Improvement Cycle and strong Leadership Commitment to build the necessary trust with external partners.
